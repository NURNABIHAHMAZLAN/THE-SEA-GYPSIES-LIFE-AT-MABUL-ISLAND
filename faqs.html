Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. Why are they called Sea Gypsies and where can we find them?

+ -

They are known as ‘Sea Gypsies’ due to their nomadic and seafaring way of life. They may be found in Mabul, Bohey Dulang, Sibuan, Maiga, Mantabuan, Selakan & Omadal.

2. What is the current population?

+ -

The sea gypsies have roamed the waters of the Coral Triangle between Malaysia, the Philippines, and Indonesia for many generations. A great number have given up their nomadic lifestyle to settle permanently on land in search of greater stability that is known as the Bajau Darat but many still retain their gypsy-esque lifestyle and the number of Bajau Laut living around Semporna is estimated to be upwards of 3000.

3. What is their lifestyle?

+ -

The sea gypsies rarely, if ever, set foot on land. When they do spend the occasional night or two on solid ground, they often report feeling ‘landsick’.

Preferring the sea, they would only go ashore for business. This may include selling their catches, collecting fresh water for drinking or wood for making boats and as well, to bury the dead. Most would return to their boats by nightfall.

4. What is their occupation?

+ -

They hunt for fish, lobsters, sea cucumbers (tripang), and other marine life. They earn their living solely based on the ocean’s resources.

5. What skills do they master?

+ -

The sea gypsies are exceptional free divers. Many have mastered the art of freediving to depths of well over 20m while holding their breath for several minutes.

Apart from being excellent at fishing and diving, they are also skilled at constructing pile houses and boats called lepa. Beautifully decorated, technicolor lepa boats feature in the popular Regatta Lepa festival.

6. What is the status of the Sea Gypsies people now?

+ -

Today, the area has transformed into a bustling tourist destination attracting divers and holiday-goers from the world over. However, the plight of the sea gypsies has remained largely unchanged. They have always been and still are, ‘stateless’ people.

According to the United Nations, there are more than 10 million stateless people in the world. Most are not afforded citizenship status by either Malaysia, the Philippines, or Indonesia, as they have been deemed to reside in the waters of the Sulu and the Celebes Sea, and not strictly on land owned by any of the countries.

7. What is Regatta Lepa?

+ -

Regatta Lepa water festival showcases the customs and traditions of the ‘Sea Gypsies‘. The highlight includes a floating parade of colourful lepa boats, lepa boat racing and tug-of-war, beauty pageant, and fireworks. Takes place annually around mid to end of April and lasts 2-3 days.
